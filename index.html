Principles of Operation of ADSL

ADSL (Asymmetric Digital Subscriber Line) technology is based on high-speed data transmission over traditional copper telephone lines. It utilizes the higher frequency band of the spectrum available on these lines, which is typically not used for voice communications. This allows telephone service and Internet access to coexist on the same line without interference (Concepto.de, n.d.).

1. Frequency and Band Division

  • Voice Band (POTS): Occupies the lowest frequency band (0 to 4 kHz), used for traditional telephone communications (POTS - Plain Old Telephone Service) (Wikipedia, n.d.).
  • Data Band (ADSL): Occupies the rest of the spectrum, typically between 26 kHz and 1.1 MHz. Within this band, the signal is divided into smaller frequency subchannels:
    • Upstream Frequency: Between 26 kHz and 138 kHz, used for transmitting data from the user to the Internet provider (upload) (Calilio, n.d.).
    • Downstream Frequency: Between 138 kHz and 1.1 MHz, used for downloading data to the user (download) (Calilio, n.d.).

This separation ensures that users can make and receive phone calls while browsing the Internet without interference (Muy Tecnológicos, n.d.).

2. DMT (Discrete Multi-Tone) Modulation

ADSL uses a modulation technique called DMT (Discrete Multi-Tone), which divides the bandwidth into subchannels or tones of 4.3 kHz each. Each of these subchannels is individually evaluated to determine how fast it can transmit data based on the quality of the telephone line. If a subchannel experiences interference or noise, its capacity is automatically adjusted to improve transmission (GeeksforGeeks, n.d.).

3. Distance and Attenuation

The speed and quality of the ADSL connection depend directly on the distance between the user’s location and the ISP’s telephone exchange. The greater the distance, the higher the signal attenuation (loss of quality) (GeeksforGeeks, n.d.).

Principles of Operation of FTTH

FTTH (Fiber to the Home) technology provides Internet access through fiber optic cables that reach directly to the user’s home. Unlike traditional copper cables, fiber optic cables are made of glass or plastic, allowing for faster data transfer speeds and less interference (LovTechnology, n.d.).

1. Components of the FTTH Network

  • Internet Service Provider (ISP): Provides the Internet connection.
  • Fiber Optic Network: The infrastructure that connects the user’s home to the service provider.
  • User Equipment: The equipment used by the user to connect to the Internet, such as a router or modem (Keyfibre, n.d.).

2. Basic Operation

Fiber optics transmit information in the form of light, which travels through the cable at the speed of light, allowing for faster data transfer and less interference. The FTTH connection consists of three main parts: the ISP, the fiber optic network, and the user equipment (RedesZone, 2024).

3. Advantages of FTTH

  • Faster Speeds: Allows for significantly faster Internet speeds.
  • More Reliable Connections: Offers more stable connections with less susceptibility to interference.
  • Greater Bandwidth: Supports a higher volume of data simultaneously.
  • Lower Latency: Reduces the delay time in data transmission (LovTechnology, n.d.).
